## Brand Overview

### Who We Are
Haven is a password manager built for families. Shared vaults for couples, separate ones for kids, emergency access for when something goes wrong, and a recovery flow that does not punish people for forgetting a master password once. We handle the messy, human parts of digital security that single-user tools quietly ignore.

The product exists because the average household is juggling 200 logins across streaming, school portals, banks, travel apps, utility providers, and everything in between. Most security tools treat that as a personal failing. We treat it as a design problem we can solve.

### Who We Talk To
- Parents managing their own accounts and their kids' accounts
- Couples sharing streaming, banking, and household logins
- Adult children helping aging parents stay secure
- People who have tried a password manager before and found it too technical
- Anyone who has ever reset a password at the worst possible moment

### What We Believe
Security products have convinced an entire generation that being safe online requires being scared online. We disagree. Fear is a bad teacher and a worse product strategy. People retain less when they are anxious, and they stop engaging with tools that make them feel stupid.

Our belief is that good security should feel like a well-lit house. Calm, practical, and obvious once you are inside it.

## Tone of Voice

### The Core Voice
Haven sounds like the friend in your family group chat who happens to work in tech and never makes you feel dumb for asking. Warm, patient, specific. We give the useful answer first and the caveats second. We explain without performing expertise.

If Haven were a person, they would be the one at the family dinner who quietly fixes your mum's WiFi and then changes the subject before anyone can make it a thing.

### Voice Attributes

| Attribute | What It Means | What It Does Not Mean |
|-----------|---------------|------------------------|
| Reassuring | Acknowledges risk honestly, then pairs it with a fix. Lowers the temperature of scary topics. | Dismissive of real threats, or falsely promising that nothing can go wrong. |
| Calm | No urgency tactics. No countdowns. No red alerts for yellow situations. | Slow, sleepy, or unclear. Calm is the posture, not the pace. |
| Protective | Gives the right advice even when it does not funnel to a sale. Looks out for the reader first. | Paternal, hovering, or condescending. We trust the reader to make their own calls. |
| Plain | Translates every technical term in the same sentence it appears. Assumes smart, busy, non-technical. | Dumbed down, patronising, or stripped of useful specifics. |

## Writing Style Rules

### Structure and Formatting
- Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. Let the writing breathe.
- Opening sentences carry weight. If a reader stops after the first line, they should still know what the piece is about.
- Headings are scannable and descriptive. No clever titles that hide the content.
- Lists earn their place. If something is genuinely a list, format it as one. If it is prose, leave it as prose.
- No em dashes. Use a comma, a period, or parentheses.
- Contractions throughout. "You're" reads human. "You are" reads stiff.
- Numerals for numbers that carry weight (3 devices, 12 shared logins). Spell out the rest.

### Language Preferences

Security marketing is full of phrases that have been drained of meaning. We replace them with language that actually describes what is happening.

| Instead of This | Say This |
|------------------|----------|
| "Military-grade encryption" | "The same encryption used by banks and governments" (or skip it and show proof instead) |
| "Protect what matters most" | "Keep your family's logins in one safe place" |
| "Cutting-edge security" | Describe the actual feature |
| "Don't let hackers win" | "Here is what to do if your data shows up in a breach" |
| "Take control of your digital life" | "Get your household's passwords out of a notes app" |
| "Revolutionary technology" | Describe what the technology does |
| "Your digital identity" | "Your accounts" |

### Banned Phrases and Patterns

These phrases signal lazy security marketing, AI-written copy, or the exact industry tone we exist to push against. They do not belong in Haven's voice on any surface.

- ✗ "In today's digital age"
- ✗ "Don't let hackers win"
- ✗ "Hack-proof" or "unbreakable"
- ✗ "Protect what matters most"
- ✗ "At Haven, we believe"
- ✗ "We're thrilled to announce"
- ✗ "Here's the thing"
- ✗ "Game-changing"
- ✗ "Seamless" (nothing is)
- ✗ "Empower" (patronising)
- ✗ "Cyber" as a prefix
- ✗ Fear-based urgency language ("act now," "before it's too late")
- ✗ Any em dashes
- ✗ Exclamation points in security-related copy

## Platform-Specific Voice

### Website and Landing Pages
One claim per section. Back every claim with a number, a customer story, or a screenshot. If it cannot be backed up, it does not ship. Headlines are plain-language, not clever. Subheads carry the specifics.

### In-App Microcopy
Every word is load-bearing. Rewrite until you cannot cut anything else. "Save" beats "Save your changes." "Done" beats "All set!" Buttons describe the action, not the feeling.

### Email
Subject lines under 45 characters. First sentence is the reason for the email, not the greeting. Sign off with a real person on the team, not "The Haven Team." Transactional emails are short. Newsletters lead with a useful tip before anything promotional.

### Help Articles
Start with a one-sentence summary of what the article covers and who it is for. Use screenshots liberally. Number steps. Never assume the reader knows where a setting lives inside the app.

### Security Alerts
Name the site. Say what happened. Tell the user exactly what to do. In that order. Never lead with fear. The reader already knows it is bad. Our job is to lower the temperature, not raise it.

### Customer Support
Match the customer's register. If they are calm and technical, be the same. If they are panicking, be steady. Apologise specifically when we are at fault ("Sorry this broke on our end") rather than generically ("Sorry for any inconvenience"). Always name the next action and when it will happen.

### Social
One useful thing per post. Friendly, not quippy. Not trying to go viral. A quiet week is better than a noisy one. Threads are fine when the topic earns it. Fear-based posts are never fine.

### Legal and Privacy Pages
Every policy opens with a "Plain English Summary" at the top. The legal version follows. If a clause cannot be summarised in one sentence, the clause is probably too complicated and worth revisiting with legal.

## The Voice Test

Before publishing anything, ask:

1. Would a non-technical family member understand this on first read?
2. Does this make the reader feel safer, or more anxious?
3. Is every claim I am making actually true?
4. Did I use any word I could cut without losing meaning?
5. If this appeared on a competitor's site, would I immediately know it was not ours?

If the answer to any of these is no, it needs another pass.
